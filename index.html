##So What's A Blockchain?
##What's In A Name? - The blockchain derives its name from how it's structured. - A blockchain consists of time-stamped blocks of transactions, linked in chronological order. - Each transaction record and block is irrevocably* recorded and secured by cryptography.
##But Language Can Be Confusing - The term 'blockchain' is commonly used in a number of different ways. - Sometimes people use the term to refer to The Bitcoin Blockchain, sometimes other digital currency or tokens and sometimes they are referring to smart contracts. - However, most of the conversation has now shifted to it referring to distributed ledgers - transactions that are replicated across a number of computers. Not on a centrally stored server.
##Permissioned vs Permissionless - Most blockchains are either permissioned or permissionless. - Another way to think about this taxonomy is 'public' vs 'private' networks. * Bitcoin is like the internet and permissioned blockchains are like intranets.

##So What's The Big Deal?
##It's The Internet of Value - Just as TCP/IP changed the way information was exchanged, Blockchain tech is change the way value is exchanged. - The first use case was bitcoin. - Now value can be moved without trusted third parties.
##Decentralising Trust - Blockchain tech allows parties who don't have full confidence in each other to collaborate without the need for a centralised authority (aka a trusted third party). - Meaning parties can transact/pass information between each other (peer-to-peer) without a central authority (e.g. SWIFT).

Here are some examples...

Banking

This has been the most active sector - with every bank of note doing some form of experimentation with Blockchain tech

##Banking Examples - *November 2015*: ConsenSys demonstrated a two party total swap financial contract that used underlying identity, reputation and general ledger components. - *February 2016*: Clearmatics announced it was developing a new clearing platform for OTC derivatives that it called a Decentralised Clearing Network (DCN). Using this a consortium of clearing members could automate contract valuation, margining, trade, compression, and close-out without a centralised clearing counter party or thread party intermediation.
##Banking Examples (cont.) - *March 2016*: Forty of the world’s biggest banks demonstrated a test system for trading fixed income, using five different blockchain technologies (R3 consortium).

- **What do all these have in common?** The transactions were done from start to finish on a peer-to-peer basis, _without central intermediaries or clearinghouses in the middle_. This is the promise of blockchain in banking.

##What Are The Current Risks/Issues?
##Technical Issues - The technology is still in its infancy (see DAO as an example). - Still very few examples of projects in production. - PoCs are being worked on - and need time to gain momentum.
##Business Risks - Corporates are still trying to wrap their heads around the technology. - Still very much at a 'blockchain all the things stage'. - Very few companies have the internal capabilities to build blockchain products. - It's a 'bright shiny thing', but may prove a little to early in the adoption curve for corporates.
##Regulatory Risks - Clarity has improved in many jurisdictions. - **But** the issue is that the technology traverses so many different areas of law that it's still hard to understand all the legal ramifications. - However, generally the regulatory environment in Australia is well adapted and allows for a fair amount of experimentation with the technology.
##So What Should Corporates Be Doing?
##Internalising Learning Is Key - It's not an outsourcing problem. - Build and experiment internally with small projects. - Have an internal champion. - Business use cases first, technology second. - Integration might not be the only play. * For example, what couldn't be done before? (e.g. what does decentralisation 'buy' you?)
